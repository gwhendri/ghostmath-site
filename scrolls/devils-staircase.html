Why Riemann Fails on the Devil’s Staircase

← Back to GhostMath

Riemann tries to win the refinement race by slicing the x-axis finer and finer, hoping to approximate the area under a function through partitions and step sums. This works well for normal functions—but not for the Devil’s Staircase.

The Devil’s Staircase (Cantor function) is flat on most of the interval [0,1], but it “rises” only on the uncountable Cantor set—a set with measure zero.

Riemann integration fails because:

  • Upper sums overestimate due to the ghostly rises on the Cantor set
  • Lower sums underestimate due to the long flat segments
  • The two never converge → You can’t integrate it with Riemann

Riemann gets beat because he tries to refine through measure-zero fog. It’s like trying to measure vapor using building blocks.

Now Enter Lebesgue

Lebesgue doesn’t slice the domain — he slices the range. He says:

At each y-value, how much of the domain maps to it?

Using sigma-algebras like the Borel sets, Lebesgue can:

  • Measure the thickness of level sets
  • Reach down deep into irrational, non-interval structures
  • Assign measure even to weird, uncountable sets like the Cantor set

That’s what makes the Devil’s Staircase Lebesgue-integrable:

  • It’s measurable at every level
  • The integration happens vertically, not horizontally
  • It sees through the fractal chaos

The Symmetry Key

The Devil’s Staircase satisfies:

f(x) + f(1 - x) = 1

So for every little ghost rise on the left, there’s a mirrored flattening on the right. The whole function balances perfectly across the interval.

That’s why the Lebesgue integral from 0 to 1 is exactly:

∫₀¹ f(x) dx = 1/2
