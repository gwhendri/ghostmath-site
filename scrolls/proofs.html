Proof 1: Irrationality of \( \sqrt{n} \) for Non-Square Integers

We prove that for any natural number \( n \) that is not a perfect square, the square root of \( n \) is irrational.

Suppose \( \sqrt{n} = \frac{a}{b} \) in lowest terms, where \( a, b \in \mathbb{Z} \), \( b \ne 0 \), and \( \gcd(a, b) = 1 \).

Then \( n = \frac{a^2}{b^2} \Rightarrow nb^2 = a^2 \). This means \( a^2 \) is divisible by \( n \).

But this leads to a contradiction unless \( n \) is a perfect square — because otherwise, \( a \) would have a prime factor that \( b \) doesn’t, contradicting their minimality.

Thus, \( \sqrt{n} \) is irrational if \( n \) is not a perfect square. \( \blacksquare \)

Proof 2: Superexponential Collapse

Claim: \( \lim\limits_{n \to \infty} \frac{n^n}{\left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^{n^n}} = 0 \)

This result captures the intuition that exponentiating a base slightly greater than 1 by a massive power like \( n^n \) will dominate \( n^n \) itself in the denominator.

We can intuitively rewrite the denominator:

\[ \left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^{n^n} = e^{n^{n-1}}(1 + o(1)) \]

And since the numerator grows like \( n^n \), we are left with:

\[ \frac{n^n}{e^{n^{n-1}}} \to 0 \]

This collapse is more powerful than exponential decay — it's superexponential.
