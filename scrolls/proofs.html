1. Irrationality of \( \sqrt{n} \) for Non-Perfect Squares

Theorem: If \( n \) is a positive integer that is not a perfect square, then \( \sqrt{n} \) is irrational.

Proof: Assume \( \sqrt{n} = \frac{p}{q} \) where \( p, q \in \mathbb{Z} \), \( q \neq 0 \), and \( \gcd(p, q) = 1 \). Squaring gives: $$ p^2 = n q^2 \Rightarrow n = \frac{p^2}{q^2} $$ which implies \( q^2 \mid p^2 \).

Case 1: \( q \mid p \) Then \( p = qk \) and: $$ n = \frac{(qk)^2}{q^2} = k^2 $$ contradicting that \( n \) is not a perfect square.

Case 2: \( q \nmid p \) Then \( \frac{p}{q} \notin \mathbb{Z} \). Squaring preserves this: $$ \left( \frac{p}{q} \right)^2 = \frac{p^2}{q^2} \notin \mathbb{Z} $$ because no cancellation occurs — primes that fail to divide \( p \) still fail to divide \( p^2 \), and likewise for \( q \). But this contradicts: $$ n = \frac{p^2}{q^2} $$ since \( n \) is an integer by assumption.

Conclusion: In both cases, we hit contradiction. Thus, \( \sqrt{n} \) is irrational for all non-perfect square integers \( n \). □

2. Superexponential Collapse via Doubling Intuition

Claim: As \( n \to \infty \), $$ \frac{n^n}{\left(1 + \frac{1}{n} \right)^{n^n}} \to 0 $$

This is known by standard asymptotic tools, but we present a new model using recursive doubling intuition, avoiding Stirling’s approximation or calculus.

Observe: $$ \left(1 + \frac{1}{n} \right)^n \approx e, \quad \text{but for small } n,\quad \left(1 + \frac{1}{n} \right)^n > 2 $$ So: $$ \left(1 + \frac{1}{n} \right)^{n^n} = \left[ \left(1 + \frac{1}{n} \right)^n \right]^{n^{n-1}} \geq 2^{n^{n-1}} $$ Hence: $$ \frac{n^n}{2^{n^{n-1}}} \to 0 $$

Interpretation:

  • \( n^n \) grows superexponentially with a fixed base.
  • Denominator grows by recursive doubling over \( n^{n-1} \) layers.
  • Even small \( n \) shows collapse: $$ \frac{256}{2^{64}} \approx 1.39 \times 10^{-17} $$

Conclusion: This offers an intuitive, constructive take on asymptotic collapse using doubling logic — accessible to minds outside academia, and revealing deep structure through unconventional reasoning. □
